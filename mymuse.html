User Research & Persona Analysis

When it came to developing an understanding of the user base, I wanted to cover a comprehensive population. This meant conducting user interviews between rare social media users and frequent users. I reached out to Dheeptha, our backend engineer, Faiza, our project manager, and Abel, a friend and student. Each one of them had varying attibutes that helped them bring unique perspectives that we considered as we continued development and design.




Dheeptha

Dheeptha, our first persona, is a freshman in computer science and a frequent social media user across multiple platforms. Her primary issue revolves around social media's tendency to data-farm and collect data. Becuase of this, her experience feels more scrutinized. Furthermore, social media seems to focus more on toxicity more recently than content from genuine creators. That being said, she would want to see an app featuring all the interactivity of typical social media while introducing one of her interests, music. Furthermore, she wants a more authentic platform that focuses less on the "fakeness" of social media, which ties into a niche interest like music.

Faiza

Faiza, our second persona, is similar to Dheeptha. She has more responsibilities in her everyday life, but apart from these, is also a frequent social media user. Her primary pain points are also fixated towards making a new platform more niche to her interests and less focused on monetization, and ties into Dheeptha's goals for a more authentic platform. Though these personas don't seem to differ as much, they allow insight on whether similar problems are picked up on by more and less frequent users.

Abel

Finally, we have Abel, a student and friend who is a rare user of social media for anything apart from chatting. He has multple apps downloaded, but they are primarily used for communication purposes. Abel's pain points include a lack of personalized content, much like Dheeptha and Faiza, and the recent emphasis of social media's use in politics and controversy. His goals, as someone who only uses social media for chatting, is to have a platform focused on a specific interest and focus more on making it entertaining rather than open-for-use.

Research → Solution

Lucky for us, there were quite a lot of overlapping goals across all of our target users, so we decided to create MyMuse, an app focused on sharing a 'song-of-the-day' to friends.


"Music is a way to bond with your friends, but there are hardly any forms of social media that allow you to interact with your friends in that sense."
— Faiza Rahman, Project Manager

Between all personas, an emphasis was placed on trying to stray away from typical, influencer-type posts and interactions. This is why, for MyMuse, we opted to focus on the social part of social media instead of the media itself. We ensured that all typical interactions were still present, including commenting, liking, and adding/finding friends. As mentioned earlier, many users had an interest in a niche interest of theirs being the primary focus of a platform, and we repeatedly had mentions of music. Therefore, we decided to integrate music into our app, hence the name MyMuse. Users can upload a song of their choosing and play snippets of their own and others' song choices. We also made sure to leave these features open for potential monetization, as we planned to eventually provide up-and-coming artists a specific platform for their songs to receive royalties.

Design Process

The project began by analyzing our goals of revolutionizing a new music/social media app and taking branding guidance from already popular companies and personas. After conducting user research, I took the lead on designing the branding and wireframes for MyMuse. The particular fact that drove me to choose our final color palette was the consistency of colors. Most social media and music apps have focused on having a mono theme (using shades of black and white) with a bold accent color to emphasize the primary functions of the app. We chose purple to avoid taking commonly used colors and place a subtle emphasis on the modernity and sleekness of our apps. Competitive Analysis

For our branding, I chose competitors with slightly similar functionality and palette, as these competitors would be most similar to the eye. I cross-referenced Spotify, Twitter, Youtube Music, and Shazam to review how they utilized color in their apps. For example, Spotify and Shazam rarely use their accent colors (green and blue, respectively) in their overall design, though we associate the key actions and branding with that color. On the contrary, Twitter and Youtube Music can be seen using their accent colors (baby blue and red, respectively) frequently throughout the app. This drew attention to some of the key functionality that set each of them apart as apps. Based on this separation, I chose to follow in line with Twitter and Youtube Music and incorporate our accent color, deep purple, throughout the app in key areas such as navigation, buttons, and interaction.

As for competitors in functionality, I chose competitors based on personal experience and user research. For the most commonly used music apps in our team and community, we had Apple Music and Spotify, and for social media, we had Instagram and BeReal. However, we chose to remove Apple Music from our list, as they serve a more niche group requiring a subscription to use their music services. Additionally, our music functionality utilized Apple iTunes API, so their key benefits were automatically integrated into MyMuse as well.

Our final lineup was Spotify, BeReal, and Instagram. BeReal has similar interactivity with its user as MyMuse, Spotify is one of the most popular music apps, and Instagram is one of the most popular social media apps with music functionality added in. However, we highlighted how none of these apps addressed the primary features we wanted from a new social media platform. Challenges

Tech Stack

One of the first roadblocks we faced was related to our tech stack. We originally started with JavaScript as our programming language and React Native as our framework, but due to difficulties while setting up our environments (and Dart/Flutter flexibility in mobile apps), we switched to our current tech stack four weeks into our project timeline, which gave us less time to develop MyMuse.

Music API Compatibility

Many music APIs we found provided documentation for more common languages like Node.js and Python, so it took some time to find proper Flutter documentation to implement a music API into MyMuse.

User Data Retrieval

In the beginning stages of development, we ran into trouble setting up the structure of the databases MyMuse would have, such as the user database. Important features of a user like a username, profile picture, followers, and following are all stored in their database and we had some trouble with data retrieval and communication with Firebase. These issues pushed back our timeline, as that caused one of our main functionalities to become a setback.

Redirecting User Flow

Finally, one of the roadblocks the front-end team faced was the user flow. We found that a lot of the original flow we had planned was redundant and easy to navigate to on a page that would typically be accessed by users. We simplified the navigation bar and ensured the adjusted user flow was visible and clickable throughout the app so that we didn’t sacrifice experience for simplicity.

Future Developments

First on the agenda is to implement a monthly song-of-the-day playlist, enabling users to see their past month of listening summarized. Next is a mood-based song of the day recommendation–based on the mood reported by the user, MyMuse will be able to suggest music. We also have a planned feature where users will be able to send song recommendations for songs they like directly to their friends. Finally, we aim to implement a feature where each user is given a monthly song color based on the songs you mused that month. For example, if a user posted more sad songs that month their monthly color would be blue.

Personal Takeaways

As the primary designer of the branding and wireframes, I was lucky that my learning process for Figma included practicing with recreating/designing mobile apps. Therefore, I didn’t struggle with transitioning between designing for a web app (the WMWE Website) to designing for a mobile app. Having started with React and web apps for development, though, I hit a learning curve initially with the nested structure that Flutter uses compared to React Native’s HTML-based structure. That being said, I had very good documentation to support my learning, and I was able to code the loading screen, navigation bar, add and request friends page, and user profile page.
